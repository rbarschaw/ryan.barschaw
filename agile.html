Agile Transformation

Leading Agile/SAFe adoption across a 100+ person engineering team. Making delivery predictable instead of chaotic.

Role Senior Program Manager
Company Microsoft Partner Center
Team Size 100+ engineers
Recognition Top 1% of org

The Situation

Partner Center's engineering organization had grown fast. What worked for a small team—informal coordination, ad-hoc planning, heroic individual efforts—was breaking down at scale.

  • Delivery was unpredictable. Leadership couldn't trust estimates. Teams couldn't trust each other's timelines.
  • Dependencies were invisible. Teams would discover blocking dependencies late, causing cascading delays.
  • Quality suffered. Rushing to meet deadlines meant cutting corners, which created more work later.
  • People burned out. The hero culture was exhausting. Good people left.
The real problem
This wasn't a "process" problem—it was a trust and visibility problem. Leadership didn't trust teams to self-organize. Teams didn't trust each other to deliver. Everyone was working around the system instead of with it.

My Approach

I helped lead the adoption of Agile/SAFe practices across the organization. But the goal was never "do Agile"—it was "make delivery predictable and sustainable." We adapted the framework to fit us, not the other way around.

Started with the Basics

  • Consistent sprint cadence across teams (2 weeks) so dependencies could be planned and handoffs were predictable
  • Sprint planning that actually resulted in achievable commitments—not wish lists that nobody believed
  • Retrospectives that led to real changes. Every retro ended with 1-2 specific action items, assigned to specific people, with deadlines.
  • Demo culture so progress was visible. Every sprint ended with a demo to stakeholders. No slides allowed—show working software.
  • Definition of Done that was explicit and enforced—code reviewed, tested, documented, deployable

Introduced PI Planning

PI (Program Increment) planning brought 100+ people together quarterly to plan the next 3 months. The format:

  • Day 1 morning: Business context and priorities from leadership
  • Day 1 afternoon: Team breakouts to draft plans
  • Day 1 end: First draft presentation of team plans
  • Day 2 morning: Dependency mapping across teams
  • Day 2 afternoon: Risk identification and mitigation planning
  • Day 2 end: Commitment to the plan

For the first time, dependencies were visible up front instead of discovered mid-sprint. Teams could see who was waiting on whom and plan accordingly.

The first few PI planning sessions were rough—people weren't used to this level of coordination. But within a few quarters, teams started to trust the process. The "surprise dependency" that derails a sprint became rare.

Made It Stick

The hardest part wasn't introducing practices—it was making them stick. Every transformation has a "trough of disillusionment" where people want to go back to the old way. I focused on:

  • Coaching teams through the awkward phases. Sitting in on standup, helping with estimation, facilitating hard conversations.
  • Protecting the process from leadership shortcuts. When executives wanted to skip retros or bypass planning for "urgent" work, I pushed back. The process only works if it's followed.
  • Celebrating wins visibly. When a team delivered a hard feature on time because of good planning, we made sure everyone knew. Success stories built momentum.
  • Adjusting based on feedback. If something wasn't working, we changed it. This isn't religion—it's about outcomes.

Delivery predictability
  • Delivery became predictable. Leadership could trust timelines. Teams could trust each other.
  • Dependencies were visible. Problems surfaced in planning, not in production.
  • Quality improved. Less rushing meant fewer production issues.
  • People were happier. Sustainable pace replaced hero culture.

I was recognized in the top 1% of the organization for driving execution and cross-team alignment. Not for a single launch, but for years of consistent, sustainable delivery.

What I Learned

  • Process is a tool, not a goal. The goal is predictable, sustainable delivery. Agile is one way to get there.
  • Transformation is cultural, not procedural. You can't just install Scrum. You have to change how people think about work.
  • Protect the process. Leadership will always have "urgent" reasons to skip retros or compress planning. Say no.
  • Celebrate small wins. Change is hard. People need to see progress to keep going.

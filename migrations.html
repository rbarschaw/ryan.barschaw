Zero-Downtime Cloud Migrations

Moving thousands of partners from German and US Gov clouds to global infrastructure. Everyone said it was impossible without downtime. We did it anyway.

Role Senior Program Manager
Company Microsoft Partner Center
Timeline 2018 – 2020
Outcome Zero downtime

The Challenge

Microsoft was consolidating regional cloud instances (Germany, US Government) into the global Azure infrastructure. This meant migrating thousands of Cloud Solution Provider (CSP) partners and their customers—live production workloads—without service interruption.

The complexity was enormous:

  • Multiple systems involved: billing, provisioning, identity, support, licensing—each with its own data model and migration requirements
  • Partners had active customers who couldn't afford downtime. These were production Azure workloads generating real revenue.
  • Different regulatory requirements across regions—data residency, compliance certifications, and audit trails all had to be preserved
  • No rollback: Once migrated, partners couldn't go back. We had one chance to get it right.
  • Coordination across time zones: Partners, Microsoft teams, and support staff were distributed globally

What Made This Hard

  • The source and destination systems had different data models—not a simple copy
  • Identity tokens had to be re-provisioned without invalidating active sessions
  • Billing had to continue uninterrupted—no gaps in invoicing or payment processing
  • Support tickets and history had to migrate with the partner
  • Partner-facing UIs had to seamlessly point to the new backend
The constraint
We couldn't do a "big bang" migration. Partners needed to continue operating while we moved them. That meant building a parallel system and cutting over seamlessly—partner by partner, in waves.

My Approach

Build the Playbook

I wrote detailed migration playbooks that every team—engineering, operations, support, partner readiness—could follow. Each playbook included:

  • Pre-migration checklist: What had to be true before we started
  • Step-by-step procedures: Exactly what to do, in what order, by whom
  • Validation gates: How to verify each step succeeded before proceeding
  • Rollback procedures: What to do if something failed (and how to know it failed)
  • Escalation paths: Who to call for each type of issue

Plan for Failure

Instead of hoping nothing would go wrong, I mapped out every failure mode we could think of:

  • What happens if identity sync fails mid-migration?
  • What if billing data is inconsistent between systems?
  • What if a partner's customer has an active support ticket during the window?
  • What if network connectivity drops during data transfer?
  • What if the destination system hits capacity limits?

For each scenario, we documented the detection method, the mitigation, and the communication to partners. We had answers before we needed them.

Run the War Rooms

During migration windows, I led cross-functional war rooms with engineering, operations, and support. The format was:

  • Status board: Real-time view of which partners were migrating, where they were in the process
  • Issue queue: Any problems surfaced immediately, triaged and assigned within minutes
  • Decision log: Every significant decision documented with rationale
  • Escalation bridge: Senior leadership on standby for major issues

We ran migrations in off-peak hours (nights, weekends) when partner activity was lowest. Everyone knew their role. There was no confusion.

Communicate Relentlessly

Partners needed to know exactly what was happening and when. I worked with partner readiness teams to ensure:

  • Partners received advance notice with specific dates and times for their migration
  • Pre-migration emails explained what to expect and what (if anything) they needed to do
  • Real-time status updates during migration windows
  • Post-migration confirmation with instructions for the new environment
  • Support contacts for any issues during the transition period

We completed the migrations with zero customer-impacting downtime. Partners continued operating throughout, and the consolidated platform was more reliable than the regional instances had been.

What I Learned

  • Planning for failure is planning for success. The teams that panic are the ones who didn't anticipate problems. Having answers ready made us fast.
  • Clear playbooks beat smart individuals. When things move fast, you need documented procedures that anyone can follow.
  • Cross-team coordination is the hardest part. The technical work was complex, but getting five teams to move in sync was harder.
  • Communicate more than you think necessary. Partners needed reassurance. Over-communication reduced support volume during migration windows.
